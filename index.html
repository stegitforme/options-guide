Section 02 — Recommended Starting Point

The Wheel Strategy

If you traded futures, you already understand leverage and risk management. The Wheel takes that discipline and applies it to the seller side of the options market — where most professional options traders actually live.

Why I'd start you here
The Wheel is mechanical, well-documented, and backtested to death. You'll build the muscle memory of selling premium, managing assignment, and thinking in probabilities — which carries over to every other strategy in this guide. It's also the strategy where futures traders most often blow up early because they don't respect theta. Start by being the seller, not the buyer.

How It Actually Works

1

Sell a Cash-Secured Put

Pick a stock you'd genuinely want to own at a discount. Sell a put 30-45 days out, at a strike 5-10% below current price (around 0.20-0.30 delta). You collect premium upfront and have cash set aside in case you get assigned.

2

If the put expires worthless

Keep the premium. Sell another put. Repeat. You're collecting income while waiting to buy the stock at your price.

3

If you get assigned

You now own 100 shares per contract at the strike price. Your effective cost basis is lower because of the premium you collected. This is fine — you wanted this stock anyway.

4

Sell Covered Calls

Now sell calls against your shares, 30-45 days out, at a strike above your cost basis (0.20-0.30 delta). Collect premium. If the calls expire worthless, sell more. If they get exercised, your shares get called away at a profit and you start the cycle over.

Stock Selection Rules

  • Only stocks you'd actually want to own. If you wouldn't be happy holding 100 shares for months, don't sell a put on it.
  • Quality over yield. A 30% IV stock paying $0.50 in premium is better than a 90% IV meme stock paying $2 — the meme stock will assign you and then keep dropping.
  • Liquid options. Tight bid/ask spreads, 200+ open interest at your strike. Garbage liquidity = garbage fills.
  • No earnings during your contract. Earnings are coin flips. Avoid them entirely.
  • Diversify across 3-5 names minimum. One bad assignment shouldn't sink the whole strategy.

Section 03

Momentum Options Strategy

This is the call-buying system I run right now. It's higher skill and more time-intensive than the Wheel — but when the setups align, the returns can be excellent. Decide for yourself if it fits your style.

A warning before you keep reading
This strategy works because of the system around it. The market filter, sector filter, position sizing math, and exit rules are not optional decorations — they ARE the strategy. Most people who try this lose money because they widen stops, hold losers, skip the screener, and treat it like discretionary trading. Read everything below before considering this.

The Big Idea

Buy call options on stocks that are already going up, in markets that are already going up, in sectors that are already going up. Don't predict tops or bottoms — surf waves that have already formed.

The whole strategy boils down to one rule: only trade when the market, the sector, AND the stock are all moving the same direction at the same time. Most retail traders pick a stock they like and hope. This flips that — you only pick stocks where everything has already aligned.

The 3-Layer Filter

Three traffic lights. ALL three must be green before you trade. Any red light, you wait.

40% of the decision

Light #1 — The Market

Before anything else: is the broad market healthy? Specifically the S&P 500 (SPY).

  • SPY trending up — 10-day EMA above 20-day above 50-day
  • SPY giving a buy signal from your indicator system
  • Fear & Greed reading under 70 and rising (room to run, not yet euphoric)
  • Market breadth confirming — most stocks participating, not just mega-caps

If SPY is in a downtrend → STOP. Park in cash equivalents. Wait.

30% of the decision

Light #2 — The Sector

If the market checks out, look at the sector the stock is in (tech, financials, energy, etc.).

  • Sector trending up with bullish moving-average alignment
  • Sector showing relative strength vs. the broader market
  • Sector breadth confirming — most stocks in the sector also trending up
  • Skip healthcare entirely — too event-driven and unpredictable for this style
30% of the decision

Light #3 — The Stock

Only after market and sector are green do you look at specific stocks.

  • Clean uptrend (10/20/50-day moving averages stacked bullishly)
  • Trading above its 10-day moving average — momentum is fresh
  • Price over $10 (cheaper stocks have garbage option pricing)
  • No earnings report in the next 4+ days
  • Stock isn't overextended — give it room to keep running, don't chase parabolic

Position Sizing — The Part Most People Skip

This is why most traders blow up. You don't buy "however many contracts feel right." You calculate it.

The formula
(Account Balance × Risk %) ÷ (2 × ATR) = # of shares
Shares ÷ Delta = # of option contracts
Risk %
How much of your account you'll lose on this one trade. Rule of thumb: 5% maximum, often less.
ATR
Average True Range — how much the stock typically moves per day. "2 × ATR" is your built-in stop loss distance.
Delta
How much the option moves per $1 of stock. Target 0.80 delta — moves ~80¢ for every $1 of stock movement.
Real example with a $100,000 account
Risk 5%=$5,000 max loss
Stock ATR = $1.50, so 2 × ATR=$3.00
$5,000 ÷ $3.00=1,666 shares-equivalent
1,666 ÷ 80 (delta)=~20 contracts
That's it. You buy 20 contracts. Not 50 because you "feel good." Not 5 because you're scared. Twenty, because that's what the math says.

Exit Rules — Half the Game

Set exits before entry, not while watching the trade.

Hard Exits (close immediately)
  • Stop loss hit — stock moves 2 ATR against you
  • Emergency exit — 3 ATR move against you intraday
  • Trend breaks — 10/20 EMA bearish cross
  • Earnings approaching — close before any announcement
  • Gap & Crap — stock gaps up 5%+ then closes back below the gap-up day's low
  • 120 calendar days held — time decay starts winning
Rolling Winners
  • Every +1 ATR move in your favor: ask "can I roll up and out for a credit?"
  • If yes — sell original, buy closer to ATM further out, pocket cash from the swap
  • This takes profit AND keeps you in the trade
  • Keep rolling until an exit signal hits

A tool, not a core holding. The Goldman Sachs Nasdaq-100 Premium Income ETF runs a covered call strategy on the Nasdaq-100 and pays roughly 10% in monthly income. Useful when the market goes flat.

How It Works

Think of it as Goldman running the Wheel for you on the Nasdaq-100:

1

Owns Nasdaq-100 stocks — same exposure as QQQ (NVDA, AAPL, MSFT, AVGO, GOOG, etc.)

2

Sells call options against a portion of the portfolio (typically 25-75% — manager has discretion based on market conditions)

3

Collects premium from those calls every month

4

Distributes the premium to shareholders as monthly income

The active overlay is the key differentiator from older covered call ETFs like QYLD (which sells 100% of the portfolio at-the-money every month and bleeds NAV over time). GPIQ's manager flexes coverage based on market view, which has helped NAV actually keep pace.

The "best" market for GPIQ is one that grinds sideways or churns up and down — exactly when buy-and-hold investors get bored.

The NAV Question

The classic knock on covered call ETFs: "they pay you back your own money — NAV erodes over time." That's mostly true for QYLD. It's not really true for GPIQ.

Since launching in October 2023, GPIQ's NAV has actually appreciated alongside paying ~10% annual distributions. The stock went from inception around $44 to $55 today, while paying out $5+ per year. Total return of ~37% over the past year — better than the S&P 500 and competitive with QQQ.

The reason: Goldman's manager doesn't sell calls on 100% of the portfolio, doesn't always sell at-the-money, and adjusts strikes based on conditions. So when underlying tech stocks rip, GPIQ captures some upside. Won't ever match QQQ in a screaming bull market — but it's not designed to.

Where It Fits

  • Monthly income without selling principal — retirees, distribution accounts, anyone wanting to live off cash flow
  • Sideways market thesis — income compounds while QQQ goes nowhere
  • Reduced-volatility Nasdaq exposure — call premiums smooth out the ride
  • Better than cash — beats treasury yields with some equity participation

Honest Tradeoffs

  • Will underperform QQQ in strong bull markets. Period. Price of the income.
  • Yield includes return of capital sometimes. Check the 19a notices. Doesn't matter for total return, matters for tax planning.
  • Still equity risk. If Nasdaq drops 30%, GPIQ drops too (just less than QQQ). Not a bond substitute.
  • Short track record. Inception Oct 2023. Hasn't been tested through a real bear market yet.
Bottom Line
GPIQ won't win the long-term return race against QQQ in a bull market. It's not supposed to. But for a sleeve designed around income and reduced volatility — especially in markets that aren't obviously trending — it's one of the better-designed products in this category. Worth keeping on the shelf as a tool, not a core position.
Section 06 — Systematic Strategy

TQQQ Vol-Target System

This isn't options — it's a rules-based weekly ETF rotation I built. The goal: capture most of TQQQ's upside while sidestepping its catastrophic drawdowns. It's beaten TQQQ buy-and-hold over 5 years with about a third of the maximum drawdown.

Why I built this
TQQQ (3x leveraged Nasdaq-100) has incredible upside in bull markets — and absolutely destroys you in bear markets. Buy-and-hold dropped -79.2% in 2022 alone. The math of leverage decay means a 50% drawdown requires a 100% recovery just to break even. This system uses simple rules to size your exposure based on market conditions, so you stay aggressive when conditions are favorable and go to cash when they aren't.

The Big Idea in One Sentence

Every Friday at market close, run a 4-step check on QQQ to decide how much TQQQ to hold for the next week. The rest goes to SGOV (short-term Treasuries) to earn ~4-5% in the cash leg. That's it.

The Rules (4 Steps, Every Friday)

1

200-Day Moving Average Gate

Is QQQ closing above its 200-day moving average? If yes, proceed to step 2. If no, go 100% SGOV (cash) and stop. This single rule is what saves you from bear-market disasters.

2

Volatility-Based Position Sizing

Calculate the position size needed to hit 35% portfolio volatility: raw_weight = (35% ÷ current_20d_vol) × 100. Lower realized vol → bigger TQQQ position. Higher realized vol → smaller TQQQ position. Cap at 75% maximum.

3

Volatility Acceleration Guard

If 20-day realized volatility exceeds 45% AND a rapid spike is detected, override and cap TQQQ at 50%. This is the backup protection against fast tape reversals that the slow-moving signals would miss.

4

Rebalance at Friday Close

Execute trades to move from current allocation to target. Round to nearest 5% for clean fills. The rest goes to SGOV. Done — wait until next Friday.

Why Each Piece Matters

The 200-Day MA
The bear-market killswitch. Single biggest contributor to outperformance vs TQQQ buy-and-hold. In 2022, this filter cut the strategy's loss to -18% while TQQQ B&H lost -79.2%.
35% Vol Target
TQQQ historically runs at 50-80% realized vol. Targeting 35% means: low-vol regimes push allocation to the 75% cap, high-vol regimes naturally cut exposure. No "panic" rule needed — the math does it for you.
The Vol Guard
Backup for fast tape reversals where both the 200MA and 20D vol are too slow to catch a sudden regime change. Forces a 50% cap during rapid spikes regardless of what the formula says.
Weekly Rebalance
Daily rebalancing on leveraged ETFs gets eaten by spreads, slippage, and whipsaw signals. Weekly cadence smooths daily noise but stays responsive to real regime changes.
SGOV as Cash Leg
Short-duration Treasury ETF earning ~4-5% in the current rate environment. Better than holding cash, no duration risk, minimal credit risk, deep liquidity.

The Honest Tradeoffs

1
You leave gains on the table in roaring bulls. 2021: system captured 47% vs TQQQ's 84.6%. 2023: system made 62.7% vs TQQQ's 193%. Vol targeting is insurance — and insurance has a cost.
2
You miss the first leg up off bear-market bottoms. Coming out of 2022, the strategy was in cash until QQQ broke back above the 200MA. By the time it re-engaged, much of the easy bounce was over.
3
Whipsaw risk. The 200MA can flip on/off in choppy markets, generating round-trip trades that cost spread + slippage.
4
Gap risk. Friday-close signal misses Monday gaps. If QQQ gaps down 5% on Monday, you're holding Friday's allocation until next Friday.
5
Short backtest history. 5 years isn't long. The system hasn't been stress-tested through a 1987-style crash, the 2000-2002 NASDAQ collapse, or 1970s stagflation. TQQQ itself didn't exist for those events.

Step-by-Step

  1. Set a Friday reminder — calendar alert for ~30 minutes before market close (3:30 PM ET).
  2. Pull the data — grab QQQ's close, 200-day moving average, and 20-day realized volatility from any charting platform (TradingView, ThinkorSwim, etc.).
  3. Calculate the target allocation using the 4-step rules above.
  4. Rebalance in your brokerage: buy/sell TQQQ and SGOV to match the target percentages of your account.
  5. Done — wait until next Friday and repeat.

Implementation Notes

  • Tax treatment: Weekly rebalances generate short-term capital gains. The strategy is structurally better in tax-advantaged accounts (IRA, Roth IRA) where rebalancing doesn't trigger tax events.
  • Brokerage: Any retail brokerage works. Both TQQQ and SGOV have deep liquidity and tight spreads.
  • Multi-account scaling: The signal is the same regardless of account size — just apply the target percentages to each account's value.
  • Position sizing: Whatever account balance you're running, apply the target percentages to that full balance. Don't half-size it "just in case" — that defeats the math.
Bottom line on this one
If you want leveraged Nasdaq exposure but can't stomach losing 80% in a bear market — which is what TQQQ B&H actually did in 2022 — this system gives you a way to participate in the upside while having a real protection mechanism. It's not magic, it leaves money on the table in screaming bulls, and it won't catch every regime change perfectly. But over 5 years it's done what it's supposed to: more return than TQQQ B&H, drawdown comparable to plain SPY.
